Resume My Work About

Public Company Equity Transactions

Exercising stock options and selling stock for public employees.

First step of a "Cashless exercise and hold" transaction.

Overview

Carta is a B2B SaaS company that helps companies and investors manage their cap tables, valuations, investments, and equity plans.

Role: Product designer

Responsibilities: Design, gathering product requirements, some front-end code

Tools: Sketch, Google Sheets

Team: 2 product designers, 4-5 engineers, product manager

Platform: Web

Timespan: September 2017 to May 2018 (~8 months)

My team was tasked with building equity administration support for public companies. Essentially, our goal was to take some of our existing private company features and rebuild them for public companies. These features include tracking and managing employee equity, exercising of stock options, and selling stocks.

Up to this point, Carta only supported private companies. Public company support was a high priority to prevent customer churn.

The team built support for stock option exercises and sales for public employees. With this functionality, we hoped to retain large, enterprise company customers as they go public.

Results and metrics (as of January 2020)

• Gained 30 new public company customers.

• Over 9000 stock option exercises by public stakeholders.

• Maintained large private companies as customers.

• Users can now:

• Exercise their public stock options.

• Sell their public stock.

• View a portfolio of their equity.

• View that statuses and history of orders.

Problem

Carta lost large, private company customers as they IPO due to a lack of functionality for public companies.

As these companies went public, and as Carta could no longer support them, they would move off the platform to other equity admin solutions, resulting in Carta losing their biggest customers.

Solution

Support public company employees by allowing them to exercise options and sell stock on Carta.

Employee portfolio. Here the user can view their equity holdings and take actions, such as exercising their option grants and selling their stock.

Transaction experience for "Cashless exercise and hold". Shares are sold to cover the exercise cost and the remaining shares are kept.

Process (highlights)

Challenge: Lack of user research

All we really knew about our users while designing was that many of them would be employees at large public companies who probably know little-to-nothing about equity.

We didn’t take the time to do formal usability testing or interviews with prospective users, and without any dedicated user research teams or processes it didn't feel feasible to do so.

Despite the lack of specific information, it was still helpful for me to empathize with a low equity IQ user. Luckily, this was easy because I was still relatively new to the equity world at the time. It was easy to think to myself “Would I find this helpful?” while working out the design.

This exercise helped me make key additions to the design, including:

1. Displaying helpful, relevant information and explaining actions.

2. A tax breakdown for each transaction.

3. A transaction history where the user can view order status.

A quick and easy way I got some signal on the design's effectiveness was testing within the company and team.

Testing with our internal equity experts was especially helpful because they had prior experience with other equity administration and trading platforms. They had valuable insight into how other platforms handled the transaction experience and made meaningful suggestions.

Challenge: Development Speed

As customer’s IPO dates approached, we worked at an increasingly fast pace. The loss of large, enterprise customers was significant, as many of them were recognizable names that strengthened the Carta brand and their contracts were high value.

This situation led to some tradeoffs in the design, namely the amount of time we could spend researching and iterating versus implementing.

With our goals in mind, we sought to keep the design as simple as possible without excluding helpful context and information for the user. Reusing existing UI components and patterns where it made sense allowed us to decrease development time and deliver value more quickly.

Implementation

Having some knowledge and experience with front-end web development allowed me to jump in and make updates to the front-end code where needed. This allowed me to ensure the front-end was polished without taking additional time from the engineers.

Challenge: Maintaining relationships with customers

Part of the team’s strategy in maintaining our pre-IPO customers was meeting with them and discussing what we needed to build to keep them on the platform post-IPO.

Maintaining these relationships was a high priority, but ultimately constrained the time and effort we could spend developing the real product. Our requirements changed often, making it difficult to plan for the long term. A percentage of our time was spent ensuring we had something interesting to demo each meeting.

I kept a tight feedback loop with our PM and engineers to ensure they understood the latest changes and updates to the designs. At the very least, this helped the team stay on the same page and move a little more quickly.

Reflection

In the end, we were able to keep some of our biggest enterprise customers and even gain some new ones. I was happy the team was able to deliver something given how constrained we were on time. I found that proactivity within the team was highly valuable in progressing with this project.

What I would have done differently

Talk and test with users! In hindsight, it would have been easy to test the product with and interview our user type, which was simply any equity-holding public company employee. This additional work would have allowed me to generate more design ideas and help swiften decision-making around the UI/UX.

Get more value out of customer meetings: We met with our customers regularly, but we didn’t seem to get much value out of them in regards to helpful information to inform development. I wish we had sought more information or context from our customers to better inform our decision-making.

Generate more ideas: When designing the transaction experience, I felt like I followed our existing UI patterns a little too closely, in order to save on development time. This choice resulted in a more consistent experience across the platform, but may not have resulted in the best user experience. If I could go back, I would spend more time generating a breadth of ideas and looking at comparable experiences in other apps. If it made sense, I would try to make the case to implement these designs that may not be consistent with other areas in the app.
